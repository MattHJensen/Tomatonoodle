## Phase II: The Ornamental Period (1650-1880)

### 300 Years as "Inedible" Decorations

For approximately three centuries, tomatoes in China were grown primarily as:
- Garden ornamentals
- Curiosity plants
- Medicinal specimens (topical use)

### Why Were Tomatoes Rejected as Food?

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Nightshade family** | Related to known poisonous plants |
| **Foreign origin** | Cultural suspicion of "barbarian" foods |
| **No culinary tradition** | No existing recipes or preparation methods |
| **Taste profile** | Acidity unfamiliar to Chinese palate |
| **Appearance** | Bright red color associated with danger in nature |

### Lao She's "The Tomato" (è¥¿çº¢æŸ¿, 1935)

**Primary Source Document**

This essay by one of China's greatest modern writers captures the cultural moment of tomato adoption:

**Key Passages (translated):**

> "When the Western red persimmon first arrived, people looked at it with the same suspicion they might show a strange foreigner walking down the street."

> "My mother would not touch it. She said anything that red must be poisonous, like the berries children are warned not to eat."

> "But the young people, ah, the young people have no such fears. They eat it raw, sliced, with a little sugar. They say it is refreshing."

> "I have come to appreciate the Western red persimmon. Not because I have become Western, but because I have learned that good food recognizes no borders."

**Historical Significance:**
- Documents generational divide in food acceptance
- Captures exact moment of cultural transition
- Provides primary source evidence of attitudes toward "foreign" food
- Written by canonical literary figure, preserved in archives

### The Tomato-Egg Combination Emerges

**Earliest Known Documentation: Late 1930s-Early 1940s**

The combination of tomato and egg appears to have emerged from:
1. **Western influence** - Eggs commonly paired with tomatoes in Western cooking
2. **Chinese adaptation** - Stir-fry technique applied to Western ingredients
3. **Practical factors** - Both ingredients cheap, accessible, quick-cooking
4. **Nutritional complementation** - Perhaps intuited: eggs lack vitamin C, tomatoes provide it
