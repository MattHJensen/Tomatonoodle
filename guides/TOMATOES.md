## Preparation Techniques

### Method 1: Boiling Water Blanch (å¼€æ°´çƒ«æ³•)

**Best for:** Large batches, reliable results

```
1. Score an X on the bottom of each tomato
2. Prepare ice bath
3. Blanch in boiling water 30-60 seconds
4. Transfer to ice bath
5. Peel when cool enough to handle
```

**Pros:** Quick, works on any quantity
**Cons:** Requires boiling water, ice bath

---

### Method 2: Open Flame Method (ç«çƒ§æ³•)

**Best for:** 1-2 tomatoes, gas stove users

```
1. Insert chopstick or fork into stem end
2. Hold over open flame, rotating
3. Continue until skin blisters and cracks
4. Cool briefly, peel off skin
```

**Pros:** No extra pots needed
**Cons:** Only for gas stoves, 1-2 at a time

---

### Method 3: Spoon Scraping (å‹ºå­åˆ®çš®æ³•)

**Best for:** Ripe tomatoes, no heat needed

```
1. Use back of spoon
2. Firmly scrape entire tomato surface
3. Skin will loosen from flesh
4. Peel off in strips
```

**Pros:** No heat, preserves fresh texture
**Cons:** Only works on very ripe tomatoes

---

### Method 4: Freezing (å†·å†»æ³•)

**Best for:** Batch prep, very ripe tomatoes

```
1. Freeze whole tomatoes for 3+ hours
2. Remove from freezer
3. Run under warm water briefly
4. Skin slips off immediately
```

**Pros:** Easy batch processing
**Cons:** Changes texture (fine for soup)

---

### Method 5: No Peel (ä¸å»çš®)

**Best for:** Rustic style, nutrition

Some cooks prefer to leave the skin on:
- More fiber and nutrients
- Rustic texture
- Less prep work
- Cut into smaller pieces to minimize skin texture

## The "ç‚’å‡ºæ²™" Technique

This is the most important technique for tomato noodle soup.

### What is "æ²™" (ShÄ)?

Literally "sand" - refers to the grainy, thick texture that develops when tomatoes are properly cooked down. The cellular structure breaks down, releasing pectin and creating body.

### How to Achieve It

```
Step 1: Add oil to hot wok (medium heat)
Step 2: Add tomato pieces
Step 3: Add pinch of salt (draws out moisture)
Step 4: Press with spatula
Step 5: Cook 3-5 minutes until:
        - Oil turns red
        - Tomatoes are paste-like
        - No watery liquid remains
```

### Signs of Success

| Visual Cue | Chinese | Description |
|------------|---------|-------------|
| Red oil | çº¢æ²¹ | Oil separates and turns red |
| Paste texture | ç³ŠçŠ¶ | No longer chunky |
| Slight caramelization | å¾®ç„¦ | Edges barely browning |
| Glossy appearance | å‘äº® | Shiny from released pectin |

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Result | Fix |
|---------|--------|-----|
| Heat too high | Burns before softening | Use medium heat |
| Not enough oil | Sticks to wok | Use 2 tbsp minimum |
| Skipping salt | Takes longer | Add salt early |
| Not pressing | Stays chunky | Use spatula actively |
| Adding water too soon | Watery, not "æ²™" | Be patient |

## Flavor Balancing

### The Sweet-Sour Balance

Chinese tomato egg noodle soup should have a balanced sweet-sour flavor. Adjust based on your tomatoes:

| Tomato Type | Likely Balance | Adjustment |
|-------------|----------------|------------|
| Winter hothouse | Bland | Add sugar + vinegar |
| Peak summer | Sweet | May need splash of vinegar |
| Roma/Plum | Balanced | Usually none needed |
| Canned | Acidic | Add pinch of sugar |
| Cherry | Very sweet | Add rice vinegar |

### Adjustment Amounts (per serving)

| Ingredient | Amount | Effect |
|------------|--------|--------|
| White sugar | 1/2 tsp | Rounds flavor, enhances umami |
| Rice vinegar | 1/2 tsp | Brightens, cuts richness |
| Tomato paste | 1 tsp | Deepens tomato flavor |
| Ketchup | 1 tbsp | Adds sweet-tangy depth |
